HushAsk vs Slido: Anonymous Q&A for All-Hands vs Everyday Slack

Slido is built for the room — live questions and upvotes during the all-hands. HushAsk is built for the other 364 days. A practical breakdown of where each one fits, and why the two get confused.

What Slido does well

Slido is a live audience-interaction tool — now part of Cisco — built for the moment a lot of people are looking at the same screen. During an all-hands, a webinar, or a conference session, the audience submits questions, everyone upvotes the ones they want answered, and the presenter works down the list from the top. Polls and word clouds run alongside the Q&A, and the whole thing plugs into the slide deck and the video call so a moderator can drive it without leaving the presentation.

The thing Slido does best is big-room moderation. When two hundred people could ask a question and only fifteen minutes exist to answer, upvoting is the right mechanism — it surfaces what the room actually cares about instead of whatever lands in the queue first. The presenter gets a ranked list, a moderator can group duplicates, and the awkward dynamic of who-raises-their-hand disappears. For a company all-hands where leadership wants real questions rather than the same three extroverts, this is a genuine improvement over a live mic, and the option to ask without a name attached gets more honest questions into the queue.

Slido is also event-shaped, and that is a feature, not a flaw. A session has a start and an end. The Q&A belongs to that session. When the meeting closes, the event closes with it. For the job Slido is built for — the live room — that scoping is exactly right.

Where the meeting-scoped model ends

The limit shows up the day after the all-hands. The questions that matter most are often the ones nobody wanted to type into a shared screen with the whole company watching the upvote count climb — and the ones that only occur to someone on a Tuesday two weeks later, with no event on the calendar to attach them to.

Slido is designed around the event, so there is no standing channel between events and no two-way thread after one. The presenter answers a question live, the session ends, and if the asker has a follow-up there is nowhere to put it. The exchange was one-to-many in a room, not one-to-one over time. That is the correct shape for a town hall and the wrong shape for an ongoing conversation, because an ongoing conversation needs a place to live when no one is presenting.

This is the gap teams run into: they adopt Slido for the all-hands, it works well, and then they reach for the same tool to handle the feedback that arrives between all-hands — and find it was never meant to sit there waiting. The event-scoped design that makes Slido good in the room is the same design that ends at the door.

What changes with year-round anonymous Q&A

HushAsk is the channel that stays open between the events Slido runs. It is anonymous, two-way Q&A in Slack — a single channel where a leader receives anonymous messages and replies anonymously in-thread, on conversation timescale rather than event timescale. A message can arrive on a Tuesday afternoon and get a reply that same day, with a follow-up after that, because the thread does not close when a meeting does.

The other difference is the anonymity model. HushAsk replaces the Slack user identifier with a 64-character SHA-256 hash at send time. On Free, Pro, and Business+ Slack plans there is no path back to the original sender — the anonymity is cryptographic, not a policy promise that an admin will choose not to look. (On Slack Enterprise Grid plans, Slack's own audit logging records bot DM activity as a separate layer that HushAsk cannot suppress; a team on Grid should know that going in.) This is a different guarantee than asking-without-a-name inside a live event, where anonymity is a setting on a session rather than a property of how the message is stored.

Neither model is universally better — they answer different questions. Event-scoped anonymity is right for surfacing live questions in a room. Always-on cryptographic anonymity is right for the conversation that has to keep going after the room empties out.

When to pick which — and the case for both

Pick Slido when the shape of what you need is the live event. You are running an all-hands, a webinar, or a conference session, you want ranked audience questions in real time, and you want polls and word clouds driving engagement from the front of the room. For that job, a Slack-based tool is not a substitute, and HushAsk does not pretend to be one — it has no presenter mode, no upvoting, no slide integration, and is not trying to.

Pick HushAsk when the conversation you need is the one the event does not capture: the question that occurs to someone with no all-hands on the calendar, the follow-up to an answer that deserved more than a live thirty seconds, the thing an IC will only say if the message cannot be traced back. That conversation needs a place to live year-round and a stored anonymity guarantee, not a session that opens and closes.

The pattern that works for teams running both: Slido for the all-hands itself — the ranked questions, the live polls, the big-room moment — and HushAsk for the 364 other days, when the questions still come but the room is gone. The two do not overlap. The mistake is stretching either one across the whole job. Slido owns the event. HushAsk owns the everyday. Both have work to do, and neither does the other's.
